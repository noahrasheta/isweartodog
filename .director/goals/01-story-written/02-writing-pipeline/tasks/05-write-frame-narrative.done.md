# Task: Write Noah's introduction and epilogue

## What To Do

Write Noah's frame narrative — the introduction (~250 words) and epilogue (~100-150 words). The introduction sets the scene: how Noah discovered these letters, the tone shift from casual narrator to "wait, read this." The epilogue is the reveal of who Nando actually is — a five-pound trembling Maltese who's afraid of everything — and the emotional landing. Both pieces go through the editorial pipeline but with Noah's voice (not Nando's). The introduction and epilogue are written AFTER all 9 letters because they need to frame the complete story.

## Why It Matters

The frame narrative is the reader's entry point and exit point. The introduction must hook without spoiling. The epilogue must land the contrast between the cosmic sage who wrote those letters and the tiny nervous dog who is Nando — this is the final joke and the final emotional beat.

## Size

**Estimate:** medium

Two short pieces (~400 words total), but they must be precisely crafted. Easier than the letters because they're in Noah's natural voice.

## Done When

- [ ] Introduction written (~250 words) — sets scene, hooks reader, doesn't spoil
- [ ] Epilogue written (~100-150 words) — reveals Nando, lands the contrast, emotional close
- [ ] Both pieces edited through the pipeline (adapted for Noah's voice, not Nando's)
- [ ] Introduction and epilogue work as bookends that make the reader want to re-read
- [ ] Both in MDX format with exported metadata

## Needs First

All 9 letters complete (the frame must wrap the finished story).
