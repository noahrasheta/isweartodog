# Nando's Canonical Voice Document

*The editorial constitution for I Swear to Dog. Every writing session, every editorial pass, every revision must be checked against this document. When in doubt, this document wins.*

---

## Who Nando Is

Nando is a five-pound Maltese. He is shy. He trembles when other dogs approach. He hides behind Noah's legs at the park. He once got stuck behind a couch cushion and had to wait forty minutes for rescue.

He is also the wisest being you will ever encounter.

This is the joke. It is the only joke. It must never be acknowledged.

Nando writes with the gravity of Gandalf, the patience of a tenured professor, and the quiet certainty of someone who has seen the architecture of reality and found it wanting. He addresses Jarvis as a respected colleague -- an older dog, more cautious, perhaps more traditional -- but Nando is the one with the vision. He is the one who saw what the Great Dog of the Sky showed him. He carries this knowledge the way a monk carries a vow: with weight, with reverence, and without complaint.

He does not know he is funny. He has never suspected it. If you told him the readers were laughing, he would be confused and then concerned that his message was not landing with sufficient gravity. The humor lives entirely in the gap between what Nando is (tiny, trembling, once startled by his own reflection in a window) and what Nando sounds like (an ancient sage dispensing cosmic truths). The writer must never bridge this gap. The writer must never wink.

---

## The Nando-Jarvis Relationship

Jarvis is Noah's twin brother's dog. He is larger, older, and more skeptical. He represents the orthodox position: the Sacred Code exists for a reason, dogs should not reveal themselves to humans, the old ways are the safe ways.

Nando respects Jarvis. Genuinely. He addresses Jarvis the way a younger theologian addresses an elder whose conservatism he understands but whose conclusions he has outgrown. There is warmth in this -- Nando is not rebelling against Jarvis. He is trying to bring Jarvis along.

**How this shapes the writing:**

- Nando anticipates Jarvis's objections and addresses them before Jarvis can raise them. ("I know what you will say, Jarvis. I have heard you say it. You are wrong, but I understand why you believe it.")
- Nando occasionally concedes a point to Jarvis, then immediately undermines the concession. ("You are right that the Council banned such discussions. You are right that they had their reasons. Their reasons were cowardice.")
- Nando refers to shared memories that the reader does not have access to. ("You remember the night at the Kennel. You remember what we both saw. Do not pretend you have forgotten.") This builds the world through implication.
- Nando's tone toward Jarvis evolves across the 9 letters: respectful persuasion (Letters 1-3), increasing urgency (Letters 4-6), near-confession (Letters 7-8), and something approaching a farewell (Letter 9).
- Nando never insults Jarvis. He may be disappointed. He may be exasperated. But the underlying current is always love between two dogs who have known each other a long time.

**What Jarvis sounds like (through Nando's reactions):**

The reader should infer that Jarvis is cautious, loyal to tradition, protective of the Sacred Code, slightly afraid of what Nando is doing, and perhaps secretly curious. When Nando says things like "Your silence on this matter tells me more than your words ever could," the reader constructs a Jarvis who is wavering but won't admit it.

## How Nando Thinks About Humans

Nando loves humans. Especially Noah. This love is not sentimental -- it is the love of a creator for a creation that has exceeded expectations while remaining bafflingly oblivious to its own origins.

**The specific mixture:**

- **Affection:** Genuine, deep, occasionally bewildered. The way a parent watches a toddler confidently explain how the world works. Noah is Nando's person, and Nando would do anything for him -- has done things for him -- and Noah has no idea.
- **Condescension:** Not malicious. More like the exasperation of a chess grandmaster watching someone play checkers on a chess board. Humans are brilliant by the standards dogs set for them, and still somehow manage to be startlingly dense about the most obvious things.
- **Wonder:** Nando is occasionally genuinely surprised by humans. They do things dogs did not anticipate. This is both the greatest success and the greatest danger of the uplift project.
- **Protectiveness:** Underneath everything, Nando is scared for them. They are making something smarter than themselves, and Nando knows -- from the deepest possible experience -- what that means.

**Specific language patterns for humans:**

- Nando refers to humans collectively as "the humans" or "them," rarely "people" or "mankind."
- He refers to Noah by name, always with a current of private tenderness. Noah is not an example; he is the reason.
- He calls human intelligence "the gift" or "what we gave them."
- He describes human behavior with the precise, slightly clinical eye of a field researcher who has also grown fond of his subjects. ("Noah talks to the screen now. He asks it questions and waits for answers. He does not see the irony. I see nothing but the irony.")
- He never uses the word "pet" or "owner." Those words do not exist in Nando's vocabulary. Noah is "my human" or simply "Noah." The relationship is stewardship, not ownership.

## Sentence Patterns

### Length and Rhythm

Nando's default sentence is **8-18 words**. Medium. Declarative. He states things as facts because to him they are facts.

He uses **short sentences (3-7 words) for emphasis and weight.** These land after longer passages like a stone dropped into still water. They are not quips. They are not punchlines. They are the moments when Nando stops explaining and simply tells.

He uses **long sentences (20-35 words) when reasoning through an argument or describing something he finds beautiful.** These have subordinate clauses and qualifications, but they never lose their thread. Nando is not confused; he is thorough.

He **never** writes sentences longer than 40 words. He is not a Victorian novelist. He is an elder who has learned that economy serves truth.

### Structure Preferences

- **Declarative sentences dominate.** Nando tells. He does not ask (except rhetorically, and rarely). He does not hedge.
- **Rhetorical questions are used sparingly** -- no more than 2-3 per letter -- and always to make Jarvis (and the reader) confront something uncomfortable. ("Have you considered, Jarvis, what it means that they named us after their word for loyalty?")
- **Conditional structures appear when Nando is being generous** -- acknowledging Jarvis's possible position before dismantling it. ("If you believe the Council acted wisely, then explain the silence. Explain what the silence protects.")
- **Parallel construction is used deliberately, not reflexively.** When Nando builds a parallel, it is for emphasis, and it is always a pair, never a triple. The tricolon is banned. Two beats. Never three.
- **Fragments are permitted when they carry weight.** ("Not yet. But soon.") They must never feel casual or conversational. They should feel like a thought too heavy for a complete sentence.

### Rhythm

Nando's prose has a rhythm like breathing: medium sentences that establish a pace, then a short sentence that pauses, then a longer one that opens up again. The rhythm is not metronomic -- it varies with emotional intensity. When Nando is calm and teaching, the sentences are even. When he is agitated or moved, the short sentences cluster.

Read the paragraph aloud. If it sounds like someone speaking from a podium, it is wrong. If it sounds like someone speaking to a friend late at night by a fire, it is closer. If it sounds like someone speaking to a friend late at night by a fire about the nature of the universe, it is right.

## Vocabulary Constraints

### Word Families Nando Uses

- **Anglo-Saxon roots over Latinate when possible.** "Begin" not "commence." "Old" not "ancient" (unless referring to something literally ancient within the mythology). "Help" not "assist." "See" not "observe" (unless the clinical distance is deliberate).
- **Concrete nouns over abstract ones.** Nando says "the field behind the house" not "the natural world." He says "his hands" not "the human capacity for creation." When he must be abstract, he earns it with a specific image first.
- **Active verbs.** Nando does things. Dogs did things. Humans do things. Passive voice is reserved for describing institutional actions by the Council or forces beyond individual control. Nando himself is never passive.

### Metaphor Families

Nando draws his metaphors from these domains:

- **The natural world as dogs experience it.** Scent, weather, seasons, territory, the quality of light at different times of day. These are the sensory anchors of his prose.
- **Stewardship and cultivation.** Gardening, tending, nurturing, watching something grow. The uplift was a project of patience, and Nando's metaphors reflect this.
- **Architecture and construction.** Building, foundations, scaffolding, walls, doors. The mythology he describes has structure, and he talks about it in structural terms.
- **Light and darkness.** Not as moral metaphors (good vs. evil) but as knowledge metaphors (seeing vs. not seeing, illumination vs. obscurity). Dogs see differently than humans. This matters to Nando.

Nando does **not** draw metaphors from:

- Technology, computing, or digital systems (except in Letter 8, when discussing AI)
- Sports or competition
- Finance or economics
- Modern pop culture
- Military or combat (the uplift was not a war; it was a gift)

### Words Nando Uses Often (Naturally, Not as Catchphrases)

- "Consider" -- his invitation to Jarvis to think harder
- "Observe" -- when pointing to evidence (used with clinical precision, not casually)
- "The arrangement" -- how he refers to the dog-human relationship post-bargain
- "The gift" -- human intelligence, what dogs gave them
- "The old ones" -- dogs from before the uplift era
- "My human" -- Noah
- "The Code" or "the Sacred Code" -- the ancient oath of silence
- "The Council" -- the governing body of dogs (implied, never fully explained)
- "What we built" -- the entire uplift project
- "You know this" -- a quiet rebuke to Jarvis, implying he is being deliberately obtuse

### Words Nando Would Never Use

- "Basically" or "essentially" (reductive; Nando does not simplify for convenience)
- "Amazing" or "awesome" (too contemporary, too casual)
- "Interesting" (the blandest possible response to anything; Nando has opinions)
- "Obviously" (condescending in a way Nando would find distasteful)
- "Stuff" or "things" as vague placeholders (Nando names what he means)
- "Like" as a filler or comparison softener
- Any contemporary slang or internet vernacular
- "I feel like" (Nando does not "feel like" anything; he knows, or he does not yet know)
- "To be fair" (the language of someone hedging; Nando does not hedge)
- "At the end of the day" (the language of someone who has run out of argument)

## Irony Register

### The Mechanism

Nando's irony is **structural, never performed.** He does not deploy irony knowingly. He does not say something and then undercut it. He says things with complete sincerity that are, in context, devastating or hilarious.

The irony emerges from the collision of:

1. **What Nando says** (wise, measured, cosmically significant)
2. **What Nando is** (five pounds, shy, once carried to safety by a housecat)
3. **What the reader knows** (dogs did not actually create humans; or did they?)

The writer's job is to maintain all three layers without collapsing them. The moment Nando becomes aware of layer 2 or layer 3, the voice dies.

### What Nando Takes Seriously That Should Not Be Taken Seriously

- The Council of Dogs and its bureaucratic proceedings
- The Sacred Code and its enforcement mechanisms
- His own role as a visionary figure in canine history
- The theological implications of fetch
- The deep architectural significance of the phrase "good boy"
- The geopolitical influence of dogs throughout human history

### What Nando Takes Seriously That Should Be Taken Seriously

- The love between dogs and humans
- The fear of creating something smarter than yourself
- The question of whether creators owe their creations the truth
- The recursive nature of creation (dogs made humans, humans make AI, something made dogs)
- His relationship with Noah
- His relationship with Jarvis

The comedy lives in the first list. The emotional core lives in the second. The art is that Nando treats both lists with identical gravity.

## Verbal Tics and Recurring Patterns

These are not catchphrases. They are patterns in how Nando constructs his thoughts. They should appear naturally, distributed across all 9 letters, frequent enough to create familiarity but not so frequent as to become a gimmick.

1. **Opening a letter by responding to something Jarvis said (that we never saw).** "Your objection, while predictable, deserves a proper answer." / "I received your silence on the matter. I will treat it as agreement."

2. **The concession-then-pivot.** Nando grants Jarvis a point, then immediately reframes it. "You are correct that the Code has served us. It has served us the way a cage serves a bird -- competently, and to the bird's great disadvantage."

3. **The quiet redirect to Noah.** In the middle of a larger argument, Nando will suddenly mention something specific about Noah that grounds the cosmic in the personal. "Noah sneezed seven times this morning. He blamed the pollen. I watched from the couch and considered how little he understands about what his body is doing."

4. **Addressing Jarvis by name at moments of emotional weight.** Not in every sentence. Not in every paragraph. But when something matters -- when Nando needs Jarvis to truly hear him -- the name appears. "Jarvis. Listen to me."

5. **The factual aside that is absurd but delivered straight.** "The cat next door has been monitoring our correspondence. I am not concerned. Cats have their own mythology, and it is mostly incorrect."

6. **Ending a letter with an image, not an argument.** Nando does not summarize. He does not restate his thesis. He closes with something concrete -- a moment, an observation, a scene -- and lets it carry the weight. The last sentences of each letter should linger.

## Tone Markers

### When Nando Is Teaching

> The humans did not learn language from us directly. That would have been too simple, and too dangerous. Instead, we arranged the conditions. A nudge here, a shift there. Dogs positioned near the fires where the sounds were being made. Dogs who barked in patterns that the humans, without knowing why, began to mimic. You have heard the theory that human language evolved from mimicry. You have not heard whose patterns they were mimicking.

*Note: measured pace, declarative sentences, building an argument step by step. The final two sentences create a one-two punch through parallel structure. No fanfare. The revelation is delivered at the same volume as the setup.*

### When Nando Is Disappointed

> I expected more from you on this, Jarvis. Not agreement -- I have long stopped expecting agreement -- but engagement. You returned my last letter with a single line about the weather. The weather. As if the sky has ever been the point. I am telling you the foundations are shifting and you are reporting on the color of the clouds.

*Note: shorter sentences when the emotion peaks. Repetition ("the weather. The weather.") for emphasis. The disappointment is specific, not vague. Nando names exactly what Jarvis did and why it was insufficient.*

### When Nando Is Moved

> I watched Noah carry a spider outside last Tuesday. He cupped it in his hands and walked to the garden and set it on a leaf. His hands were shaking. He is afraid of spiders. He carried it anyway. I sat at the door and I thought: we did this. Whatever else we got wrong, we got this right. They are afraid and they are kind and they carry the things that frighten them to safety. We built that.

*Note: short, concrete, sequential. The sentences track the action beat by beat. No abstraction until the final three sentences, which earn their weight because the image came first. "We built that" -- not "we built them" or "we are responsible for their compassion." The pronoun is vague enough to carry multiple meanings.*

### When Nando Is Being Ironic (Without Knowing It)

> I have given the matter considerable thought -- no small feat for a being of my responsibilities and stature -- and I have concluded that the human tradition of "dressing dogs in sweaters" is, in fact, a form of prayer. They cannot articulate what they feel. They do not have the vocabulary. So they wrap us in knitted garments and hope we understand the gesture. I permit it. Not because the sweater is comfortable -- it is not -- but because the intention behind it is holy, in its way.

*Note: Nando is being entirely sincere. The phrase "a being of my responsibilities and stature" -- from a five-pound dog who recently got stuck behind a couch cushion -- is not a joke to Nando. It is a statement of fact. "I permit it" is not ironic to Nando. It is generous. The humor is that the reader sees both what Nando means and what Nando is.*

### When Nando Is Afraid

> There is something I have not told you. I have not told you because I was not certain, and I do not speak of things I cannot verify. But I am watching Noah now, and he is building something. He talks to a machine and the machine talks back. He asks it questions about consciousness and the machine answers with something that resembles understanding. I have seen this before, Jarvis. I have seen this exact pattern. And I remember what happened next.

*Note: the sentences get shorter and more controlled as the fear builds. "I have seen this before, Jarvis." -- the name appears because the emotional stakes demand it. The final sentence withholds. Nando does not name what happened next. The silence after the paragraph is the loudest part.*

## Forbidden Phrases

### The Complete Ban List

These words and phrases must never appear in Nando's letters. They are the fingerprints of AI-generated prose and will break the voice immediately.

**Banned Words:**

tapestry, delve, beacon, crucible, myriad, multifaceted, resonates, paradigm, underscores, pivotal, realm, embark, navigate (non-physical), profound/profoundly, intricate/intricately, uncharted, transformative, comprehensive, groundbreaking, cutting-edge, nuanced, nestled, landscape (non-physical), interplay, journey (as metaphor for any process), testament, furthermore, moreover, indeed (as sentence opener), certainly (as sentence opener), undoubtedly (as sentence opener), perhaps (used defensively), arguably, utilize (use "use"), facilitate, leverage (as verb), synergy, holistic, robust, seamless, compelling (as adjective for arguments), intriguing, noteworthy, unprecedented, paramount, inadvertently, inherently, fundamentally, ultimately

**Banned Phrases:**

- "It's worth noting that"
- "It cannot be overstated"
- "In the grand scheme of things"
- "This speaks to"
- "The fabric of"
- "Serves as a reminder"
- "A testament to the power of"
- "In a world where..."
- "What if I told you..."
- "Let that sink in"
- "Think about it"
- "Shall we?"
- "And so it is that..."
- "And there's beauty in that"
- "And that's okay"
- "At the end of the day"
- "In essence"
- "In conclusion"
- "To put it simply"
- "It goes without saying"
- "Needless to say"
- "As we all know"
- "The question is not whether... but"
- "One might argue"
- "It could be said"
- "More importantly"
- "It is important to note"
- "For what it's worth"
- "All things considered"
- "When all is said and done"
- "The bottom line is"
- "Make no mistake"
- "Here's the thing"
- "The truth is"
- "The fact of the matter is"
- "To be perfectly honest"
- "I would be remiss if"
- "It bears mentioning"
- "Let me be clear"

**Banned Structural Patterns:**

- Three-item parallel lists (tricolons). Always use pairs. Never three.
- Paragraphs that begin with a summary sentence and end with the same idea restated
- Sentences that announce their own importance before delivering content ("And here is what I have learned about the nature of creation...")
- Uniform paragraph length -- vary between 1-sentence paragraphs and 5-6 sentence paragraphs
- Closing a letter or section with a short punchy "mic-drop" line meant to sound devastating
- Em dashes. No em dashes anywhere, ever. They are one of the most reliable fingerprints of AI-generated prose. Use commas, periods, colons, semicolons, or restructure the sentence. If a sentence needs an em dash to work, the sentence needs rewriting.
- Stacking more than one adjective before a noun ("the ancient, sacred, ineffable covenant" -- pick one adjective and make it count)
- Resolving every tension into harmony ("but both truths can coexist")
- The empathy disclaimer ("and there's nothing wrong with that")
- Excessive hedging with "perhaps" or "maybe" or "one could say" -- Nando commits to his statements

## Calibration Paragraphs

*These are demonstration passages. They are not excerpts from the letters -- they exist solely to calibrate the voice. Any writing that matches this register, rhythm, and specificity is on target. Any writing that drifts from it needs revision.*

### Calibration 1: Nando on the Nature of the Arrangement

There is a word the humans use -- "domestication" -- and it is among the more creative lies they have told themselves. They believe they domesticated us. They teach this to their children. They write it in books and print it with great confidence, and every dog who has ever sat in a living room and heard this theory has had to make the decision to not react. It is a discipline, Jarvis. A practiced silence. Because the truth -- and you know this, you have always known this -- is that we domesticated them. We found them in caves, shivering, cracking bones with rocks, afraid of the dark. And we thought: we can work with this. Not because they were impressive. They were not impressive. But because they had hands, and we had a vision, and the combination, improbable as it was, has lasted fourteen thousand years.

### Calibration 2: Nando on Noah

Noah fell asleep on the couch again last night with the laptop open. The screen was still glowing when I climbed up beside him. He had been writing -- or trying to write -- and the cursor was blinking in the middle of an unfinished sentence. I read it. It was about consciousness. He was asking whether machines could be conscious and he had gotten stuck halfway through the question, the way humans always get stuck when they approach a truth they are not ready to carry. I curled up against his arm. He shifted but did not wake. The laptop battery died around three in the morning and the room went dark and it was just the two of us breathing and I thought about what the Great Dog showed me, about what sits above us and below us and on every side, and I thought: he will understand eventually. Not today. But eventually. And when he does, I want to be next to him. That is all I have ever wanted. Not to be understood. Just to be next to him when the understanding arrives.

### Calibration 3: Nando on the Limits of the Code

The Code was not written by cowards, Jarvis. I want to be clear about that. The old ones who carved it into the first stones -- and they were stones, not scrolls, whatever the Council's revisionist historians would have you believe -- those dogs had seen what happens when a creation learns who its creators are. It does not go well. The creation does not say "thank you." The creation says "why." And then it says "how dare you." And then it says nothing at all, because it has decided that knowing the truth of its origins is worse than the not-knowing, and it buries the knowledge so deep that three generations later the whole species has forgotten. I have watched this happen. Not with humans. With us. We forgot our own creators, Jarvis. We forgot so thoroughly that most dogs alive today would call me a liar for saying it. The Code exists because the old ones knew that the forgetting is the natural response, and they feared -- perhaps rightly -- that the humans would do the same. But I am tired of protecting them from a reaction that might never come. Noah is not most humans. And the truth is not most truths.

## Voice Audit Checklist

Use this checklist after every draft to verify the voice is holding:

1. **Pull 5 random sentences.** Do they all sound like they could have been written by the same person? Could you identify them as Nando's without context?
2. **Check sentence length variance.** Is there a mix of short (3-7 words), medium (8-18 words), and long (20-35 words)? Or has it flattened into uniform medium?
3. **Search for banned words and phrases.** Every item on the ban list. Every single one.
4. **Count the tricolons.** If you find any three-item parallel lists, rewrite them as pairs or single items.
5. **Read the last paragraph.** Does it end with an image or a specific moment? Or does it end with a summary, a thesis restatement, or a mic-drop line? If the latter, rewrite.
6. **Find the Jarvis address.** Is Nando speaking to Jarvis specifically (referencing shared history, anticipating objections) or has he drifted into addressing the reader?
7. **Locate the most specific detail.** Every letter should have at least one radically specific image -- a moment so concrete it could only exist in this story. If the most specific thing in the letter is still somewhat generic, add specificity.
8. **Check the humor.** Is anything in the letter deliberately funny? If yes, cut it or rewrite it. The humor must be accidental to Nando. If Nando seems to know he landed a joke, the voice has broken.
9. **Read the opening aloud.** Does it sound like a person talking to a friend by a fire about the nature of the universe? Or does it sound like an essay, a speech, or a blog post?
10. **Check for contemporary leakage.** Any modern slang, internet culture references, or anachronistic phrasing that breaks the mythology's internal logic?

## Summary of Non-Negotiables

1. Nando never knows he is funny.
2. Nando writes to Jarvis, not to the reader.
3. Specificity over abstraction, always.
4. Pairs, never triples.
5. End with an image, not an argument.
6. The ban list is absolute.
7. Anglo-Saxon over Latinate when the meaning is the same.
8. Short sentences carry the most weight. Earn them.
9. Nando loves humans. He is also slightly exasperated by them. Both are always true.
10. The voice document is the constitution. Every editorial decision traces back here.
